Topic 2 – Motivation 點解要讀組織工程？

Big Picture

因為好多器官唔會自己生返，捐贈又少，人等緊等緊就死晒。
Mind map: 疾病 → 無再生能力 → 器官短缺 → TE救命

Must-Know Facts

  • 🔴 香港器官捐贈率全球最低，等器官平均5年，最長29年
  • 🔴 每年需求: Skin 300萬、Bone/Cartilage/Blood vessel 各100萬、Kidney 60萬
  • 🟢 Christopher Reeve 超人演員 → 脊椎受傷終身癱瘓 → 代表性案例
  • 🔵 目標: Repair 修補 | Restore 恢復 | Regenerate 再生 | Replace 取代

Mnemonics

  • 香港三個關鍵字: 「最低、最多、最長」→ 捐贈率最低、等緊人最多、等最長

Topic 3 – History 歷史

Must-Know Timeline（背晒）

  • 1000 BC 古印度鼻移植
  • 16世紀 Tagliacozzi 用手臂皮瓣整鼻
  • 1962 首個合成皮膚
  • 1985 Langer & Vacanti 正式提出「Tissue Engineering」
  • 1996 Dolly 多利羊（第一隻複製哺乳動物）
  • 1997 Vacanti Mouse 老鼠背長人耳（PLGA + 軟骨細胞）

Mnemonics

  • 「96羊97鼠」→ Dolly 96年，Vacanti Mouse 97年

Topic 4 – Definitions 定義（必背金句）

Langer & Vacanti (1993) 最經典

「Tissue engineering is an interdisciplinary field that applies the principles of engineering and life sciences toward the development of biological substitutes that restore, maintain, or improve tissue or organ function.」
組織工程係一個跨學科領域，用工程同生命科學嘅原理，去開發可以恢復、維持或改善組織或器官功能嘅生物替代品。

三大工具 Three Tools（CSS）

  • Cells 細胞 → 活嘅部分
  • Scaffolds 支架 → 結構支撐（要可降解）
  • Signals 訊號 → 生長因子或機械刺激

TE vs Regenerative Medicine 對比

TE 組織工程Regenerative Medicine 再生醫學
In vitro 體外培養In vivo 體內直接刺激
嚴格控制環境用身體自己嘅調節

Topic 5 – Transplant Types 移植類型

四大類（背晒優缺點）

類型來源優點缺點
Autograft自己無排斥痛、來源少
Allograft其他人來源較多排斥、捐贈者少
Xenograft動物來源多排斥+感染風險
Man-made人工心/肺即用機械故障

Mnemonics

  • 「自家無痛、他人排斥、動物染病」→ Auto無排斥、Allo排斥、Xeno感染

Topic 6 – Technical Challenges 技術挑戰

四大死穴（考試最愛問）

  1. Vascularization 血管化 → 細胞離毛細血管要<100μm
  2. Scale-up 放大 → 點樣做出有臨床意義嘅大小
  3. Microenvironment 微環境 → 營養、訊號、力學
  4. Cell source & control 細胞來源同控制分化/增生

Mnemonics

  • 「血管、放大、微環境、細胞」→ 血大微細

Topic 7 – Successful Applications 成功案例

Skin 皮膚（最成熟）

  • Epicel → 自体角質細胞薄片（超薄，易碎）
  • Apligraf → 同種異體（纖維母細胞+角質細胞），3星期培養，有真皮層

Cartilage 軟骨

  • Carticel → 自体軟骨細胞注射
  • Hyalograft-C → 細胞+支架，效果更好

Bladder 膀胱（Atala 2006）

  • 首個成功移植實驗室培養嘅器官（用自体細胞+可降解支架）

Topic 8 – Advanced & Future 未來技術

  • 3D Bioprinting 生物3D打印
  • Organ-on-a-Chip 器官晶片（藥物測試，減少動物試驗）
  • Decellularized ECM 去細胞基質（Badylak）
  • Microfluidic vascular networks 微流控血管網

Topic 1 – Motivation for Tissue Engineering

Big Picture (1 sentence + mind map in text)

Tissue engineering addresses the critical shortage of organs and tissues for transplantation by creating viable substitutes.
Mind map: Need → Diseases (SCI/Parkinson’s/Alzheimer’s/Heart) → Shortage (Waitlists/Deaths) → Limited Regeneration → TE Solutions

Must-Know Facts (max 12 bullet points)

  • 🔴 Millions suffer from damaged tissues (e.g., Spinal Cord Injury, Parkinson’s)
  • 🟢 Most tissues don’t regenerate spontaneously after damage
  • 🔴 HK organ shortage: Lowest donation rates, >3,000 waiting, avg 5-29 years
  • 🟢 Waitlist: 1 added every 10 min; only 80 transplants/day
  • 🔴 Annual needs: Skin 3M, Bone/Cartilage/Vessel 1M each, Kidney 600K, Liver/Nerve 200K
  • 🟢 Examples: Christopher Reeve (SCI from accident)
  • 🔵 Congenital abnormalities, injuries, diseases drive need
  • 🟢 TE aims: Repair/Restore/Regenerate/Replace

Key Formulas / Diagrams (use code blocks)

Comparison Table (“X vs Y” in proper Markdown table)

Regenerating vs Non-Regenerating TissuesYes (Regenerate)No (Don’t)
ExamplesBone, EpidermisCardiac Muscle, Nerve
ReasonsStem cells presentLimited vasculature/proliferation
ImplicationsSelf-heal small defectsNeed TE/transplants

Common Mistakes & Examiner Traps

  • Assuming all muscles regenerate (skeletal/cardiac don’t fully)
  • Forgetting stats like annual procedures – trap: vague answers without numbers
  • Mixing HK-specific shortage with global waitlists

Mnemonics / Memory Hooks

  • Needs: “Skinny Bones Never Bleed Lots, Can’t Keep Bladder Strong” – Skin, Bone, Nerve, Blood vessel, Liver, Cartilage, Kidney, Bladder, Skeletal Muscle
  • Waitlist: “10 min add, 80 day transplants” – 10-80 rule

Past Exam Questions + Model Answers (use > blockquote for answers)

Why is there a need for tissue engineering? Give examples.

Due to limited regeneration and organ shortages causing deaths; e.g., Spinal Cord Injury (no repair), HK waits up to 29 years for organs.

What tissues regenerate spontaneously?

Bone, epithelia (e.g., epidermis); not cardiac muscle or nerves.

Topic 2 – History of Tissue Engineering

Big Picture (1 sentence + mind map in text)

Tissue engineering evolved from ancient transplants to modern cell-based therapies, driven by medical needs.
Mind map: Ancient (1000 BC nose) → 16-19th C (Skin/Tooth) → 20th C (Transplants/Synthetics) → 1980s (TE Concept) → Milestones (Dolly/Vacanti)

Must-Know Facts (max 12 bullet points)

  • 🔴 Ancient: 1000 BC Sushrutha nose transplants
  • 🟢 16th C: Tagliacozzi forearm flap for nose
  • 🔴 17-18th C: Tooth/Skin/Corneal transplants
  • 🟢 19th C: Sterile technique/anesthesia enable surgery
  • 🔴 20th C: Kidney/Heart/Lung/Bone marrow post-1950
  • 🟢 1962: First synthetic skin
  • 🔴 1985: TE articulated; 1988 first symposium
  • 🟢 Dolly (1996): First cloned mammal, sparks reproduction ideas
  • 🔵 Vacanti Mouse (1997): PLGA scaffold with chondrocytes
  • 🟢 Pet cloning available; human cloning ethical issues
  • 🔵 Shift to cell-based therapies late 20th C

Key Formulas / Diagrams (use code blocks)

Comparison Table (“X vs Y” in proper Markdown table)

Ancient vs Modern TEAncientModern
MethodsAutografts (nose/skin)Scaffolds/Cloning/3D Printing
FocusReconstructionRegeneration/Organ substitutes
TechManual surgeryCells/Biomaterials/Signaling

Common Mistakes & Examiner Traps

  • Confusing Dolly as human cloning (sheep, 1996)
  • Forgetting ethical concerns in human cloning – trap: ethical Qs
  • Misattributing Vacanti Mouse to human application

Mnemonics / Memory Hooks

  • History: “Nose To Tooth, Skin Cornea, Sterile Transplants” – N(1000BC), T(17C), S/C(18C), S(19C), T(20C)
  • Milestones: “Dolly Vacanti” – D(Cloning), V(Scaffold)

Past Exam Questions + Model Answers (use > blockquote for answers)

Outline the history of tissue engineering from ancient to modern times.

Ancient: 1000 BC nose; 16-19th C skin/tooth; 20th C transplants; 1962 synthetic skin; 1985 TE concept; 1997 Vacanti Mouse.

What was the Vacanti Mouse and its significance?

Mouse with human ear-shaped cartilage grown on back using PLGA scaffold and chondrocytes; popularized TE.

Topic 3 – Definitions and Paradigms

Big Picture (1 sentence + mind map in text)

Tissue engineering applies biology and engineering to create substitutes that restore tissue function.
Mind map: Definitions → Langer/Vacanti (1993) → Goals (Restore/Maintain/Improve) → Paradigm (Cells/Materials/Factors)

Must-Know Facts (max 12 bullet points)

  • 🔴 Definition (Langer/Vacanti): Biological substitutes to restore/maintain/improve function
  • 🟢 Fung: Understanding structure-function in tissues
  • 🔴 Laurencin: Repair using biomaterials/cells/factors
  • 🟢 Includes cells/tissues/organs/blood
  • 🔵 Paradigm: Cells + Biocompatible materials + Biochemical/Biophysical factors
  • 🟢 TE vs Regenerative Med: In vitro (TE) vs In vivo (RM)
  • 🔴 In vitro advantages: Evaluation/control; disadvantages: Remodeling needed
  • 🟢 In vivo: Physiologic environment; disadvantages: Mechanical stresses

Key Formulas / Diagrams (use code blocks)

Comparison Table (“X vs Y” in proper Markdown table)

TE vs Regenerative MedicineTissue EngineeringRegenerative Medicine
EnvironmentIn vitroIn vivo

DisadvantagesNeeds remodelingDislodgement/stresses

Common Mistakes & Examiner Traps

  • Quoting only one definition – trap: Compare multiple
  • Confusing TE as only for tissues (includes organs/cells)
  • Ignoring biophysical factors in paradigm

Mnemonics / Memory Hooks

  • Definitions: “Langer Vacanti Fung Laurencin” – LVFL (Love Very Fine Laundry)
  • Paradigm: “Cells Scaffold Signals” – CSS (Cascading Style Sheets for tissues)

Past Exam Questions + Model Answers (use > blockquote for answers)

Define tissue engineering according to Langer and Vacanti.

An interdisciplinary field that applies engineering and life sciences to develop biological substitutes that restore, maintain, or improve tissue function.

Compare TE and regenerative medicine.

TE: In vitro, controlled but needs integration; RM: In vivo, natural but prone to stresses.

Topic 4 – Strategies and Tools

Big Picture (1 sentence + mind map in text)

TE strategies combine cells, scaffolds, and signaling to engineer functional tissues.
Mind map: Tools → Cells (Function/Repair) → Scaffolds (Support/Degrade) → Signaling (Proteins/Mechanical) → Strategies (Isolated Cells/Growth Factors/Cell-Matrix)

Must-Know Facts (max 12 bullet points)

  • 🔴 3 Tools: Cells (living/protein-producing), Scaffolds (structural/biodegradable), Signaling (proteins/mechanical)
  • 🟢 Strategies: Isolated cells/substitutes, Tissue growth factors, Cell-matrix with scaffolds
  • 🔵 Paradigm considerations: Cells, Materials, Biochemical factors (growth), Biophysical (loading)
  • 🟢 Isolated cells: Infusion without surgery
  • 🔴 Growth factors: Targeted delivery/gene systems
  • 🟢 Scaffolds: Grow/replace biodegradable matrix

Topic 5 – Types of Transplants

Big Picture (1 sentence + mind map in text)

Transplants vary by source, each with benefits and drawbacks in compatibility and availability.
Mind map: Types → Autograft (Self) → Allograft (Same species) → Xenograft (Other species) → Man-made (Artificial)

Must-Know Facts (max 12 bullet points)

  • 🔴 Autograft: From self; painful, limited source
  • 🟢 Allograft: From another human; donor scarcity, rejection
  • 🔴 Xenograft: From animals; biocompatibility issues, infection risk
  • 🟢 Man-made: Artificial hearts/lungs; no rejection but mechanical failures
  • 🔵 Drawbacks: Autograft (pain/failure in elderly), Allograft (immune), Xenograft (contamination)
  • 🟢 Examples: Skin grafts, organ transplants

  • Claiming autografts have rejection (they don’t)
  • Forgetting man-made as a type – trap: Include in answers
  • Overstating xenograft safety

Past Exam Questions + Model Answers (use > blockquote for answers)

Compare types of transplants.

Autograft: Self, no rejection but painful; Allograft: Human, rejection; Xenograft: Animal, infection.

What are drawbacks of xenografts?

Biocompatibility, immune rejection, higher contamination risk.

Topic 6 – Technical Challenges

Big Picture (1 sentence + mind map in text)

TE faces hurdles in cell biology, materials, microenvironment, and scale-up for clinical use.
Mind map: Challenges → Cell (Differentiation/Proliferation) → Materials (Porosity/Degradation) → Microenv (Signals/Nutrients) → Scale (Vascularization/Automation)

Must-Know Facts (max 12 bullet points)

  • 🔴 Cell Biology: Control diff/prolif, adhesion, immune, environment
  • 🟢 Materials: 3D scaffolds, porosity vs strength, degradation, interactions
  • 🔴 Microenvironment: Reconstitute for function (cells/ECM/signals)
  • 🟢 Scale-up: Enough microenvs, microcirculation (capillaries <100um)
  • 🔵 Automation for clinical scale
  • 🟢 Cell communication/migration
  • 🔴 Post-implant: Slow vascularization, nutrient lack
  • 🟢 Vascular need: Cells near capillaries

Common Mistakes & Examiner Traps

  • Ignoring microenvironment dynamics
  • Underestimating vascularization – trap: Why thick tissues fail
  • Forgetting automation for scale

  • Challenges: “Cells Make Micro Scale” – CMMS (Comms)
  • Microcirculation: “<100um from capillary” – 100 rule

Past Exam Questions + Model Answers (use > blockquote for answers)

What are key technical challenges in TE?

Cell control (diff/prolif), materials (porosity/degrade), microenvironment (signals), scale-up (vascular/automation).

Why is vascularization a challenge?

Cells need nutrients <100um from capillaries; slow ingrowth post-implant.

Topic 7 – Specific Tissue Applications

Big Picture (1 sentence + mind map in text)

TE has succeeded in avascular tissues like skin, cartilage, bladder, but struggles with complex organs.
Mind map: Apps → Skin (Epicel/Apligraf) → Cartilage (Microfracture/Carticel) → Bladder (Autologous cells/scaffold) → Others (Kidney/Liver challenges)

Must-Know Facts (max 12 bullet points)

  • 🔴 Skin: Epicel (autologous keratinocytes), Apligraf (dermal/epidermal)
  • 🟢 Skin repair: Epithelial regen, Dermal matrix (Alloderm), Full-thickness
  • 🔴 Limitations: Slow vasc, no glands/follicles
  • 🟢 Cartilage: Limited regen; Microfracture, Osteochondral transplants
  • 🔴 Cell-based: Carticel (autologous chondrocytes)
  • 🟢 Scaffold: Hyalograft-C; better in young/athletes
  • 🔴 Bladder: Autologous cells on scaffold; integrated over years
  • 🟢 Success: Avascular tissues; hurdles for vascular (kidney/liver)
  • 🔵 Kidney: Glomerular structure hard
  • 🟢 Liver: Hepatic lobules challenging

15-Minute Mock Exam

  1. Define tissue engineering and explain its paradigm.

    Interdisciplinary field developing substitutes to restore function; involves cells, materials, biochemical/biophysical factors.

  2. Compare autograft and xenograft.

    Autograft: Self-source, no rejection but painful/limited; Xenograft: Animal, abundant but infection/rejection risk.

  3. What are challenges in cartilage TE?

    Limited regeneration, inefficient redifferentiation, scaling thickness, cell sources.

  4. Describe Apligraf for skin repair.

    Allogeneic fibroblasts in collagen + keratinocytes; 3 weeks, promotes healing despite rejection.

  5. Why vascularization is key challenge?

    Nutrient lack post-implant; cells need <100um from capillaries.

  6. Name a pioneer and contribution.

    Langer: TE concepts and controlled release systems.
