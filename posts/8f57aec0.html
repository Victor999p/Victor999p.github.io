🧠 Nanomaterials: Origins & Impact

Historical Foundations

  • The concept of nanotechnology was popularized by Richard Feynman in his 1959 lecture “There’s Plenty of Room at the Bottom”.
  • Feynman used vivid storytelling to advocate for atomic-scale engineering, inspiring decades of research.
  • Many of his predictions have since been realized through modern nanotechnology.

Evolution of Medical Applications

  • Initial breakthroughs (1980s–1990s):
    • PEGylation for drug stability
    • Targeting ribosomes
    • Endometriosis treatments
  • Current technologies:
    • Lipid and ceramic nanoparticles
    • Polymer micelles and protein carriers
    • Cell-based and viral delivery systems

🔬 Key Properties of Nanomaterials

Surface-to-Volume Ratio

  • As particle size decreases, the proportion of surface atoms increases:
    • ~90% for single-atom layers
    • ~35% for 7-layer clusters
    • <5% for 30 nm particles
  • This enhances chemical reactivity and biological interaction.

Size-Dependent Behavior

  • Nanomaterials exhibit unique properties not seen in bulk materials.
  • Operate in a transitional zone between molecular and macroscopic scales.
  • Enable novel applications in medicine, electronics, and energy.

🟡 Gold Nanoparticles & Localized Surface Plasmon Resonance (LSPR)

Discovery & Physical Properties

  • First observed by Michael Faraday in 1857: wine-colored gold colloids
  • Gold nanoparticles have a lower melting point than bulk gold
  • Exhibit Localized Surface Plasmon Resonance (LSPR):
    • Oscillation of conduction electrons in response to light
    • Responsible for vivid colors (e.g., red from 520 nm green light absorption)

Applications

  • Biosensors: Fluorescence quenching for detection
  • mRNA detection: Using detachable linkers
  • Protein biomarkers: Enhanced sensitivity via plasmonic effects

Tuning LSPR

  • Size Increase → Red shift in absorption
  • Shape Control (e.g., nanorods) → Longitudinal resonance in far-red region
  • Aggregation → Red shift due to interparticle coupling

🌈 Quantum Dots (QDs): Color by Size

Overview

  • Semiconductor nanocrystals with size-dependent emission
  • Recognized by the 2022 Nobel Prize

Mechanism

  • Smaller QDs → Larger band gap → Emission at shorter wavelengths (blue)
  • Larger QDs → Smaller band gap → Emission at longer wavelengths (red)

Manufacturing

  • Synthesized at ~300°C using cadmium, selenium, and sulfur
  • Stabilized with ZnS shells and organic ligands

Advantages

  • Photostability: Resistant to photobleaching
  • Broad absorption with narrow emission
  • Used in QLED displays, solar cells, and bioimaging

🌐 SPR vs LSPR Technologies

FeatureSPR (Surface Plasmon Resonance)LSPR (Localized SPR)
Evanescent Field250 nm – 1 μmMuch shorter
Propagation LengthMicrons to millimetersLocalized
Resonance ConditionsPrism opticsLight wavelength
  • SPR: Surface wave at metal-dielectric interface
  • LSPR: Electron cloud oscillation around nanoparticles
  • LSPR explains the color of gold nanoparticles

🧪 Liposomes & Lipid Nanoparticles (LNPs)

Liposomes

  • Discovered by Alec Bangham in the 1960s
  • Composed of a double lipid bilayer enclosing an aqueous core
  • Can encapsulate both hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs
  • Common components: phospholipids, PEG-lipids, cholesterol
  • Fabrication: freeze-drying → hydration → size control

Lipid Nanoparticles (LNPs)

  • Solid lipid-based particles for nucleic acid delivery
  • Fabricated via microfluidics
  • Typical formulation: 12 mg/mL lipids, 25% ethanol
  • Purification: tangential flow filtration (TFF), dialysis, size exclusion

🧭 Nanomaterial Fabrication Strategies

Bottom-Up Approaches

  • Chemical synthesis: e.g., gold nanoparticles via citrate reduction
  • Self-assembly: lipid vesicles, micelles
  • Spray drying: for drug particle formation

Top-Down Approaches

  • PRINT® technology: Particle Replication in Non-wetting Templates
  • Photolithography: Inspired by microelectronics
  • Graphene exfoliation: Electrochemical peeling

Surface Stabilization

  • Charge-based (e.g., citrate): unstable in salt solutions
  • Steric-based (e.g., PEG): preferred for stability and protein corona prevention

💊 Nanomedicine: Benefits & Challenges

Benefits of Nanoparticle-Based Drug Delivery

  • Improved bioavailability and solubility
  • Suitable for intravenous (IV) administration
  • Extended circulation time in the body
  • Enhanced tissue penetration
  • Targeted accumulation at disease sites

Design Considerations

  • Size:
    • 150 nm → accumulation in liver, lungs, spleen
    • <10 nm → rapid clearance via kidneys
  • Shape:
    • Disc-shaped particles → liver/lung/spleen targeting
  • Surface Charge:
    • Positive charge → increased uptake in liver/lung

Delivery Challenges

  • Macrophage uptake reduces circulation time
  • Non-specific organ distribution
  • Laminar blood flow limits vascular wall interaction
  • Tumor pressure hinders deep tissue penetration

PRINT® Technology Applications

  • Used by Liquidia for lung-targeted delivery
  • Particle size (1–5 μm) affects deposition depth
  • Particle shape influences biodistribution

⚡ Charge-Stabilized Nanoparticles

  • Less stable than PEG-coated particles
  • Stabilization via electric double layer:
    • Stern layer: tightly bound ions
    • Diffusive layer: loosely bound ions
  • Salt exposure disrupts the double layer → aggregation

🧬 Gene Delivery Using Nanoparticles

Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

  • DNA → mRNA → Protein

Types of Therapeutic Nucleic Acids

  • DNA: e.g., Luxturna (FDA-approved in 2017)
  • mRNA: e.g., Pfizer/Moderna COVID-19 vaccines
  • siRNA: e.g., Patisiran (FDA-approved in 2018)
  • microRNA: experimental, not FDA-approved

Delivery Methods

  • Viral Vectors:
    • Retrovirus, Adenovirus, AAV, HSV
  • Non-Viral Methods:
    • Gene gun, Electroporation, Nanoparticles

Role of Lipid Nanoparticles (LNPs)

  • Protect nucleic acids from nucleases
  • Avoid renal clearance
  • Facilitate endocytosis
  • Proposed structures: onion-like, cake-like, solid core

🧫 COVID-19 Vaccine Mechanism & Industry Trends

1. Mechanism of mRNA-LNP Vaccines

  • mRNA encodes spike protein
  • Delivered via LNPs
  • Uptaken by antigen-presenting cells (APCs)
  • Activates B cells and T cells for immune response

2. Market Trends (2023)

  • 80% of vaccines use non-lipid nanoparticles
  • Viral vectors still dominate gene delivery

Tutorial-5

Q1. Which is correct in terms of surface plasmon resonance (SPR)?

A. SPR involves valence band electrons
B. SPR involves conduction band electrons
C. SPR involves magnetic field oscillation
D. None of the above

✅ B. SPR involves conduction band electrons
Explanation: SPR is caused by the collective oscillation of conduction electrons in resonance with the electric field of incident light.

Q2. What is the difference between LSPR and SPR?

A. Substrate
B. Plasmon
C. Signal
D. All

✅ D. All

Explanation:
SPR occurs on flat metal surfaces; LSPR occurs on nanoparticles. Differences include substrate type, plasmon behavior, and signal detection.

Q3. Compare the longest SPR wavelength among gold nanostructures:

  1. 10 nm AuNPs
  2. 50 nm AuNPs
  3. 10×60 nm nanorods
  4. 10×40 nm nanorods

A. 1 > 3 > 2 > 4
B. 2 > 1 > 3 > 4
C. 3 > 4 > 2 > 1
D. 4 > 3 > 2 > 1

✅ C. 3 > 4 > 2 > 1

Explanation: Nanorods with higher aspect ratios show longer SPR wavelengths. Size also contributes, but shape dominates.

Q4. Which nanoparticles change color when 3 M NaCl is added?

A. Citrate-stabilized 20 nm AuNPs
B. PEG-stabilized AuNPs
C. CdSe QDs with PEG
D. Lipid nanoparticles with PEG

✅ A. Citrate-stabilized 20 nm AuNPs

Explanation: Salt disrupts electrostatic stabilization in citrate-coated AuNPs, causing aggregation and color change.

Q5. Quantum dots emitting blue, green, and red light—compare retention time in size exclusion chromatography:

A. Blue > Green > Red
B. Green > Red > Blue
C. Red > Green > Blue
D. Red > Blue > Green

✅ A. Blue > Green > Red

Explanation: Smaller QDs (blue) elute slower due to more pore interaction. Larger QDs (red) elute faster.

Q6. What method can be used to make liposomes?

A. Electrochemical exfoliation
B. PRINT technology
C. Spray drying
D. Self-assembly

✅ D. Self-assembly

Explanation: Liposomes form spontaneously when amphiphilic lipids self-assemble in water.

Q7. Major difference between liposomes and lipid nanoparticles?

A. Size
B. Surface functional group
C. Composition and function
D. Surface charge

✅ C. Composition and function

Explanation: Liposomes have bilayer structures; LNPs are solid or amorphous. Their composition and delivery roles differ.

Q8. Which factor influences nanoparticle biodistribution in vivo?

A. Size
B. Surface charge
C. Shape
D. All of the above

✅ D. All of the above

Explanation: All these factors affect circulation, organ targeting, and cellular uptake.

Q9. Why are lipid nanoparticles needed for nucleic acid drug delivery?

A. In vivo degradation by nucleases
B. Renal clearance
C. Negative charge prevents membrane crossing
D. All of the above

✅ D. All of the above

Explanation: LNPs protect nucleic acids, improve delivery efficiency, and prevent rapid clearance.

Q10. mRNA-1273 (Moderna COVID vaccine) is injected intramuscularly.

  • Role of mRNA: Provides genetic instructions to antigen-presenting cells (APCs) to produce the spike (S) protein antigen.
  • Role of lipid nanoparticles (LNPs): Protect mRNA from degradation, facilitate cellular uptake, and help deliver mRNA into APCs.
  • Target cells: APCs such as dendritic cells and Langerhans cells translate the mRNA into spike protein, initiating the immune response.
