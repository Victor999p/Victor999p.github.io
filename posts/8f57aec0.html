Historical Foundations

ğŸ”¬ Key Properties of Nanomaterials

Surface-to-Volume Ratio

  • As particle size decreases, the proportion of surface atoms increases:
    • ~90% for single-atom layers
    • ~35% for 7-layer clusters
    • <5% for 30 nm particles
  • This enhances chemical reactivity and biological interaction.

Size-Dependent Behavior

  • Nanomaterials exhibit unique properties not seen in bulk materials.
  • Operate in a transitional zone between molecular and macroscopic scales.
  • Enable novel applications in medicine, electronics, and energy.

ğŸŸ¡ Gold Nanoparticles & Localized Surface Plasmon Resonance (LSPR)

Discovery & Physical Properties

  • First observed by Michael Faraday in 1857: wine-colored gold colloids
  • Gold nanoparticles have a lower melting point than bulk gold
  • Exhibit Localized Surface Plasmon Resonance (LSPR):
    • Oscillation of conduction electrons in response to light
    • Responsible for vivid colors (e.g., red from 520 nm green light absorption)

Applications

  • Biosensors: Fluorescence quenching for detection
  • mRNA detection: Using detachable linkers
  • Protein biomarkers: Enhanced sensitivity via plasmonic effects

Tuning LSPR

  • Size Increase â†’ Red shift in absorption
  • Shape Control (e.g., nanorods) â†’ Longitudinal resonance in far-red region
  • Aggregation â†’ Red shift due to interparticle coupling

Overview

  • Semiconductor nanocrystals with size-dependent emission
  • Recognized by the 2022 Nobel Prize

Mechanism

  • Larger QDs â†’ Smaller band gap â†’ Emission at longer wavelengths (red)

  • Broad absorption with narrow emission
  • Used in QLED displays, solar cells, and bioimaging

ğŸ§ª Liposomes & Lipid Nanoparticles (LNPs)

Liposomes

  • Discovered by Alec Bangham in the 1960s
  • Composed of a double lipid bilayer enclosing an aqueous core
  • Can encapsulate both hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs
  • Common components: phospholipids, PEG-lipids, cholesterol
  • Fabrication: freeze-drying â†’ hydration â†’ size control

Lipid Nanoparticles (LNPs)

  • Solid lipid-based particles for nucleic acid delivery
  • Fabricated via microfluidics
  • Typical formulation: 12 mg/mL lipids, 25% ethanol
  • Purification: tangential flow filtration (TFF), dialysis, size exclusion

ğŸ§­ Nanomaterial Fabrication Strategies

Bottom-Up Approaches

  • Chemical synthesis: e.g., gold nanoparticles via citrate reduction
  • Self-assembly: lipid vesicles, micelles
  • Spray drying: for drug particle formation

Top-Down Approaches

  • PRINTÂ® technology: Particle Replication in Non-wetting Templates
  • Photolithography: Inspired by microelectronics
  • Graphene exfoliation: Electrochemical peeling

Surface Stabilization

  • Charge-based (e.g., citrate): unstable in salt solutions
  • Steric-based (e.g., PEG): preferred for stability and protein corona prevention

ğŸ’Š Nanomedicine: Benefits & Challenges

Benefits of Nanoparticle-Based Drug Delivery

  • Improved bioavailability and solubility
  • Suitable for intravenous (IV) administration
  • Extended circulation time in the body
  • Enhanced tissue penetration
  • Targeted accumulation at disease sites

Design Considerations

  • Size:
    • 150 nm â†’ accumulation in liver, lungs, spleen
    • <10 nm â†’ rapid clearance via kidneys
  • Shape:
    • Disc-shaped particles â†’ liver/lung/spleen targeting
  • Surface Charge:
    • Positive charge â†’ increased uptake in liver/lung

Delivery Challenges

  • Macrophage uptake reduces circulation time
  • Non-specific organ distribution
  • Laminar blood flow limits vascular wall interaction
  • Tumor pressure hinders deep tissue penetration

PRINTÂ® Technology Applications

  • Used by Liquidia for lung-targeted delivery
  • Particle size (1â€“5 Î¼m) affects deposition depth
  • Particle shape influences biodistribution

  • Less stable than PEG-coated particles
  • Stabilization via electric double layer:
    • Stern layer: tightly bound ions
    • Diffusive layer: loosely bound ions
  • Salt exposure disrupts the double layer â†’ aggregation

ğŸ§¬ Gene Delivery Using Nanoparticles

Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

  • DNA â†’ mRNA â†’ Protein

Types of Therapeutic Nucleic Acids

  • DNA: e.g., Luxturna (FDA-approved in 2017)
  • mRNA: e.g., Pfizer/Moderna COVID-19 vaccines
  • siRNA: e.g., Patisiran (FDA-approved in 2018)
  • microRNA: experimental, not FDA-approved

Delivery Methods

  • Viral Vectors:
    • Retrovirus, Adenovirus, AAV, HSV
  • Non-Viral Methods:
    • Gene gun, Electroporation, Nanoparticles

Role of Lipid Nanoparticles (LNPs)

  • Protect nucleic acids from nucleases
  • Avoid renal clearance
  • Facilitate endocytosis
  • Proposed structures: onion-like, cake-like, solid core

Q1. Which is correct in terms of surface plasmon resonance (SPR)?

A. SPR involves valence band electrons
B. SPR involves conduction band electrons
C. SPR involves magnetic field oscillation
D. None of the above

âœ… B. SPR involves conduction band electrons
Explanation: SPR is caused by the collective oscillation of conduction electrons in resonance with the electric field of incident light.

Q2. What is the difference between LSPR and SPR?

A. Substrate
B. Plasmon
C. Signal
D. All

âœ… D. All

Explanation:
SPR occurs on flat metal surfaces; LSPR occurs on nanoparticles. Differences include substrate type, plasmon behavior, and signal detection.

âœ… C. 3 > 4 > 2 > 1

Q4. Which nanoparticles change color when 3 M NaCl is added?

A. Citrate-stabilized 20 nm AuNPs
B. PEG-stabilized AuNPs
C. CdSe QDs with PEG
D. Lipid nanoparticles with PEG

âœ… A. Citrate-stabilized 20 nm AuNPs

Explanation: Salt disrupts electrostatic stabilization in citrate-coated AuNPs, causing aggregation and color change.

  • Target cells: APCs such as dendritic cells and Langerhans cells translate the mRNA into spike protein, initiating the immune response.
